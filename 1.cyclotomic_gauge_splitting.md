## Abstract

In simplicial (Quadray) coordinate systems, the $N$-axis frame on $\mathbb{R}^N$ carries a one-dimensional gauge redundancy along $\mathbf{1} = (1, \ldots, 1)^\top$, and the geometry lives on the zero-sum hyperplane $H = \{v : \sum_i v_i = 0\}$ [Murillo-Montero 2026]. The simplicial frame admits a natural cyclic label symmetry, generated by the permutation $C : \mathbf{e}_i \mapsto \mathbf{e}_{i+1 \bmod N}$, which satisfies $C^N = I$ and preserves the gauge / zero-sum decomposition. We show that the polynomial factorization

$$X^N - 1 = (X - 1) \cdot \Phi^*(X), \qquad \Phi^*(X) = \sum_{k=0}^{N-1} X^k,$$

reproduces the gauge / zero-sum splitting *as a decomposition of $\mathbb{Q}[C]$-modules*: the linear factor $(X - 1)$ corresponds to the gauge axis $\mathbb{R}\mathbf{1} = \ker(C - I)$, and the cofactor $\Phi^*(X)$ corresponds to the action of $C$ on $H = \ker \Phi^*(C)$. For $N$ prime, $\Phi^*(X) = \Phi_N(X)$ is the $N$-th cyclotomic polynomial (irreducible over $\mathbb{Q}$), and $H_{\mathbb{Q}}$ becomes a rank-one $\mathbb{Q}[C]$-module over the cyclotomic field $\mathbb{Q}(\zeta_N)$. For $N$ composite, $H_{\mathbb{Q}}$ decomposes as a direct sum indexed by the non-trivial divisors of $N$, each summand carrying its own primitive cyclotomic structure.

A signed parity governs orientation: $\det(C|_H) = (-1)^{N-1}$, so $C|_H \in SO(N-1)$ for $N$ odd and $C|_H \in O(N-1) \setminus SO(N-1)$ for $N$ even. The cyclic label symmetry is therefore an *orthogonal permutation symmetry* of the simplicial frame, not in general a rotation; it coincides with a Rodrigues rotation only for odd $N$, and even then must be distinguished from the continuous Lie-algebraic rotations of [Murillo-Montero 2026].

The case $N = 3$ is exceptional: it is the unique value for which $C|_H$ is real-irreducible of dimension $\geq 2$. Equivalently, $N = 3$ is the only value for which the centered vertices of the regular $(N-1)$-simplex coincide, in some 2-plane, with the $N$-th roots of unity. We give the explicit identification of the cube roots of unity with the centered triangular simplicial frame at $N = 3$, exact under the simplicial Gram metric.

This paper is a *companion algebraic note* to [Murillo-Montero 2026]. Where the prior paper develops the continuous Lie-algebra structure on the gauge quotient (singling out $N = 4$ for cross-product / Rodrigues exceptionality), the present note develops the finite cyclic-group structure on the same quotient, singling out $N = 3$ for cyclotomic planar exceptionality. No physics interpretation is offered here.

**Keywords:** simplicial coordinates, Quadray coordinates, cyclic permutation, Coxeter element, type $A_{N-1}$ reflection representation, Weyl group, cyclotomic polynomial, cyclotomic field, gauge direction, zero-sum hyperplane, regular polytope vertices, roots of unity, Eisenstein integers.

## 1. Introduction

### 1.1 Setting

The $N$-frame simplicial coordinate system on $\mathbb{R}^{N-1}$ — Quadray coordinates for $N = 4$ [Urner; Ace; QuadrayWiki], barycentric coordinates more generally [Ciarlet] — uses $N$ frame vectors pointing to the vertices of a regular $(N-1)$-simplex. It is overcomplete by exactly one dimension: $N$ coordinates describe an $(N-1)$-dimensional Euclidean space. The redundant direction is the *gauge axis* $G = \mathbb{R}\mathbf{1}$, where $\mathbf{1} = (1, 1, \ldots, 1)^\top \in \mathbb{R}^N$, and the physically meaningful geometry lives on the *zero-sum hyperplane*

$$H = \mathbf{1}^\perp = \{v \in \mathbb{R}^N : \textstyle\sum_i v_i = 0\}.$$

The orthogonal decomposition $\mathbb{R}^N = G \oplus H$ is the *gauge / zero-sum splitting* introduced in [Murillo-Montero 2026]; an extensive treatment of its inner-product, cross-product, and rotation structure for $N = 4$ is given there.

The simplicial frame carries a natural finite group action: cyclic permutation of the coordinate indices, generated by

$$C : \mathbf{e}_i \mapsto \mathbf{e}_{i+1 \bmod N}.$$

$C$ is an integral orthogonal matrix of order $N$, and the cyclic subgroup $\langle C \rangle \cong \mathbb{Z}/N$ sits inside the full simplicial symmetry group $S_N$ acting by permutation matrices. The minimal polynomial of $C$ is exactly $X^N - 1$, which factors over $\mathbb{Q}$ uniquely into cyclotomic polynomials,

$$X^N - 1 = \prod_{d | N} \Phi_d(X).$$

This paper exploits that factorization to give a refined description of the gauge / zero-sum decomposition: as an algebraic identity between cyclotomic factors and invariant subspaces.

### 1.2 Main results

The principal results are:

**(Theorem 1, the cyclotomic gauge/zero-sum splitting.)** The factorization $X^N - 1 = (X - 1) \cdot \Phi^*(X)$, with $\Phi^*(X) := (X^N - 1)/(X - 1) = \sum_{k=0}^{N-1} X^k$, decomposes $\mathbb{R}^N$ as $\mathbb{R}[C]$-modules into the gauge axis $G = \ker(C - I)$ (the linear factor) and the zero-sum hyperplane $H = \ker \Phi^*(C)$ (the cofactor).

**(Theorem 2, the prime-$N$ cyclotomic field structure.)** For $N$ prime, $\Phi^*(X) = \Phi_N(X)$ is irreducible over $\mathbb{Q}$, and $H_{\mathbb{Q}} := H \cap \mathbb{Q}^N$ is a free rank-one module over the cyclotomic field $\mathbb{Q}[C]/\Phi_N(C) \cong \mathbb{Q}(\zeta_N)$, with $C$ acting as multiplication by $\zeta_N$. The identification $H_{\mathbb{Q}} \cong \mathbb{Q}(\zeta_N)$ as a field requires choosing a cyclic generator and is non-canonical.

**(Theorem 3, the $N = 3$ self-cyclotomic uniqueness.)** $N = 3$ is the unique value for which $C$ acts irreducibly on $H$ over $\mathbb{R}$ with $\dim_{\mathbb{R}} H \geq 2$. Equivalently, the centered vertices of the regular $(N-1)$-simplex coincide with the $N$-th roots of unity, in some real 2-plane, iff $N = 3$.

**(Theorem 4, composite-$N$ decomposition.)** For any $N \geq 2$, the rational zero-sum hyperplane decomposes as

$$H_{\mathbb{Q}} = \bigoplus_{d | N, d > 1} H^{[d]}_{\mathbb{Q}}, \qquad H^{[d]}_{\mathbb{Q}} := \ker \Phi_d(C)|_{H_{\mathbb{Q}}},$$

with $\dim_{\mathbb{Q}} H^{[d]}_{\mathbb{Q}} = \phi(d)$, and each summand $H^{[d]}_{\mathbb{Q}}$ is, as a $\mathbb{Q}[C]$-module, a free rank-one module over $\mathbb{Q}(\zeta_d)$.

### 1.3 Relation to prior work

This paper is a *companion algebraic note* to [Murillo-Montero 2026], not a continuation of the Lie-theoretic / Rodrigues-rotation construction developed there. The two papers analyze the same gauge quotient from complementary algebraic angles:

- [Murillo-Montero 2026]: the gauge quotient $H$ as a Euclidean / Lie-algebraic arena, carrying inner product, cross product, and the continuous Lie-algebra structure $\mathfrak{so}(N - 1)$, with the construction singling out $N = 4$ (the *cross-product / Rodrigues exception*: $\mathfrak{so}(3) \cong (\mathbb{R}^3, \times)$ admits a single-axis binary cross product).
- The present note: the same gauge quotient $H$ as the *non-trivial cyclotomic part of the cyclic permutation representation* $\mathbb{Z}/N \curvearrowright \mathbb{R}^N$, with the construction singling out $N = 3$ (the *cyclotomic planar exception*: $\Phi_3$ is the unique cyclotomic polynomial under which $H$ is real-irreducible of dimension $\geq 2$).

The cyclic operator $C$ and the continuous Rodrigues rotations of [Murillo-Montero 2026] sit on the same hyperplane but are distinct objects. In particular, $C|_H$ is a rotation in $SO(H)$ only for *odd* $N$; for *even* $N$, $\det(C|_H) = -1$ and $C|_H$ is an orthogonal but orientation-reversing transformation. Even when $C|_H \in SO(H)$, it is generally a *product* of planar rotations rather than a single-axis (Rodrigues) rotation: for $N \geq 5$ prime, $C|_H$ acts as simultaneous rotations through angles $2\pi/N, 4\pi/N, \ldots$ on the distinct real 2-planes of the cyclotomic decomposition (Theorem 2'). The single-axis case occurs only at $N = 3$, where $H$ has dimension 2 and a planar rotation is single-axis by default. The rotation content for even $N$ does not vanish — $C^2|_H$ is always in $SO(H)$, and for $N = 4$ it is the half-turn through opposite-edge midpoints of the tetrahedron — but it sits in the index-2 subgroup $\langle C^2 \rangle \subset \langle C \rangle$. The orientation-reversing content of $C$ for even $N$ is concentrated in the $\Phi_2$-component of the cyclotomic decomposition, where $C$ acts as $-I$.

Equivalently, the construction of the present paper is the cyclotomic decomposition of a Coxeter element in the type $A_{N-1}$ reflection representation, expressed in simplicial coordinates so that the gauge direction $\mathbb{R}\mathbf{1}$ remains explicit alongside the Coxeter-restricted action on $H$. The cyclic shift $C \in S_N$ realizes a Coxeter element of $W(A_{N-1}) = S_N$ acting on its standard reflection representation $H$; its eigenvalue / cyclotomic content is then classical Coxeter-element theory [Bourbaki 1968]. The novelty in the present treatment is the simultaneous bookkeeping of the gauge axis and the cyclotomic content in the simplicial frame, not the cyclotomic content itself.

The cyclotomic content of cyclic-group representations is classical [Serre 1977, §I; Lang 2002, §VI; Washington 1997]. The novelty here is the explicit identification with the gauge / zero-sum decomposition of simplicial coordinates, the consequent geometric reading of the prime-vs-composite distinction, and the precise pairing of $N = 3$ and $N = 4$ as the two distinguished frames in the simplicial framework — each exceptional from a different algebraic perspective.

## 2. Preliminaries

### 2.1 Notation

Let $N \geq 2$ be an integer. We work in $\mathbb{R}^N$ with the standard inner product $\langle \cdot, \cdot \rangle$ and standard basis $\mathbf{e}_1, \ldots, \mathbf{e}_N$. Write $\mathbf{1} = (1, 1, \ldots, 1)^\top$ and define:

- the **gauge direction** $G = \mathbb{R}\mathbf{1}$ (1-dimensional);
- the **zero-sum hyperplane** $H = \{v : \sum_i v_i = 0\} = \mathbf{1}^\perp$ ($(N-1)$-dimensional);
- the projections $P_G = \tfrac{1}{N}\mathbf{1}\mathbf{1}^\top$ onto $G$ and $P_H = I - P_G$ onto $H$.

We write $H_{\mathbb{Q}} = H \cap \mathbb{Q}^N$ for the rational points of $H$. The simplicial Gram matrix [Murillo-Montero 2026, §2.4] is $G_s = \tfrac{N}{N-1} I - \tfrac{1}{N-1} \mathbf{1}\mathbf{1}^\top$, with associated inner product $\langle v, w \rangle_s = v^\top G_s w$. On $H$ this restricts to $\langle v, w \rangle_s = \tfrac{N}{N-1} \langle v, w \rangle$; on $G$ it restricts to zero.

### 2.2 The cyclic permutation operator

Define the **cyclic permutation** $C : \mathbb{R}^N \to \mathbb{R}^N$ by $C \mathbf{e}_i = \mathbf{e}_{i+1 \bmod N}$. In matrix form,

$$C = \begin{pmatrix}
0 & 0 & \cdots & 0 & 1 \\
1 & 0 & \cdots & 0 & 0 \\
0 & 1 & \cdots & 0 & 0 \\
\vdots & & \ddots & & \vdots \\
0 & 0 & \cdots & 1 & 0
\end{pmatrix} \in \mathrm{GL}_N(\mathbb{Z}).$$

**Lemma 2.1.** *$C$ is orthogonal ($C^\top C = I$), satisfies $C^N = I$, has minimal polynomial $X^N - 1$, and has determinant $\det C = (-1)^{N-1}$. Restricted to $H$, $\det(C|_H) = (-1)^{N-1}$ as well, since $C$ acts trivially on $G$.*

*Proof.* Orthogonality and $C^N = I$ are immediate from the permutation structure. The minimal polynomial divides $X^N - 1$ because $C^N - I = 0$. The vectors $\mathbf{e}_1, C\mathbf{e}_1, C^2 \mathbf{e}_1, \ldots, C^{N-1}\mathbf{e}_1$ equal $\mathbf{e}_1, \mathbf{e}_2, \ldots, \mathbf{e}_N$ respectively, so they are linearly independent and no polynomial of degree $< N$ in $C$ annihilates $\mathbf{e}_1$; the minimal polynomial therefore has degree exactly $N$ and equals $X^N - 1$. The determinant is the sign of the cyclic permutation, which is the sign of an $N$-cycle: $(-1)^{N-1}$. $\square$

**Corollary 2.1' (orientation parity).** *$C|_H \in SO(N-1)$ for $N$ odd, and $C|_H \in O(N-1) \setminus SO(N-1)$ for $N$ even. Thus $C|_H$ is a (proper) rotation in $\mathbb{R}^{N-1}$ if and only if $N$ is odd.*

**Proposition 2.2 (rotational core of the cyclic label symmetry).** *The orientation-preserving subgroup of $\langle C \rangle$ acting on $H$ is*

$$\langle C \rangle \cap SO(N-1) \;=\; \begin{cases} \langle C \rangle & \text{if } N \text{ is odd}, \\ \langle C^2 \rangle \cong \mathbb{Z}/(N/2) & \text{if } N \text{ is even}. \end{cases}$$

*For even $N$, the orientation-reversing content of $C$ is concentrated in the $\Phi_2$-eigenspace (where $C$ acts as $-I$ and $C^2$ acts as $+I$). On the complementary cyclotomic components $\bigoplus_{d | N,\; d > 2} H^{[d]}_{\mathbb{Q}}$, the operator $C^2$ acts with the same orbit structure as $C$ but with the cyclotomic order halved on even-order components ($\Phi_{2m} \mapsto \Phi_m$).*

*Proof.* For odd $N$, Corollary 2.1' gives $\det(C|_H) = +1$, so all powers of $C$ are in $SO(N-1)$. For even $N$, $\det(C|_H) = -1$ and $\det(C^k|_H) = (-1)^k$, so the orientation-preserving powers are exactly the even powers, generating $\langle C^2 \rangle$ of order $N/2$. The cyclotomic content of $C^2$ on each $\Phi_d$-component follows from the squaring map on roots of unity: for odd $d$, the map $\zeta_d \mapsto \zeta_d^2$ permutes the primitive $d$-th roots (since $\gcd(2, d) = 1$), so $C^2|_{H^{[d]}}$ has the same minimal polynomial $\Phi_d$ as $C|_{H^{[d]}}$; for even $d = 2m$, the map sends primitive $2m$-th roots to primitive $m$-th roots, so $C^2|_{H^{[2m]}}$ has minimal polynomial $\Phi_m$. $\square$

**Example 2.3 ($N = 4$ refinement).** $C|_{H_4}$ is *not* a rotation in $SO(3)$; it is a roto-reflection with spectrum $\{-1, i, -i\}$. However, $C^2|_{H_4}$ has spectrum $\{1, -1, -1\}$ and *is* a rotation: specifically, the rotation by $\pi$ (a half-turn) about the 1-dimensional $\Phi_2$-eigenspace of $C$, with the 2-dimensional $\Phi_4$-eigenspace as its rotation plane. Geometrically, $C^2$ realizes the half-turn through the midpoints of one pair of opposite edges of the tetrahedron — an element of the Klein four-group $V_4 \subset A_4$ inside the tetrahedral rotation group. The cyclic 4-label shift $C$ behaves like a quarter-turn only after forgetting its orientation-reversing axial sign; its square is a genuine half-turn in $SO(3)$.

**Lemma 2.2.** *$C$ preserves the gauge / zero-sum decomposition: $C \mathbf{1} = \mathbf{1}$ and $C(H) = H$.*

*Proof.* $C$ permutes coordinates, so $\sum_i (Cv)_i = \sum_j v_j$. Applied to $\mathbf{1}$ gives $C \mathbf{1} = \mathbf{1}$; restricted to zero-sum vectors it gives $C(H) \subseteq H$, with equality by invertibility. $\square$

### 2.3 The cyclotomic factorization

Recall the standard factorization over $\mathbb{Q}$ [Lang 2002, §VI.3]:

$$X^N - 1 = \prod_{d \,|\, N} \Phi_d(X),$$

where $\Phi_d$ is the $d$-th cyclotomic polynomial, of degree $\phi(d)$ (Euler totient), with roots the primitive $d$-th roots of unity, and irreducible over $\mathbb{Q}$. We separate out the trivial factor $\Phi_1(X) = X - 1$ and write

$$\Phi^*(X) := \frac{X^N - 1}{X - 1} = \sum_{k=0}^{N-1} X^k = \prod_{d \,|\, N,\; d > 1} \Phi_d(X).$$

For $N$ prime, $\Phi^*(X) = \Phi_N(X)$ is itself irreducible, of degree $N - 1$. For $N$ composite, $\Phi^*$ factors further.

## 3. The cyclotomic gauge / zero-sum splitting

**Theorem 1 (cyclotomic gauge / zero-sum splitting).** *For all $N \geq 2$, the gauge / zero-sum decomposition of $\mathbb{R}^N$ coincides with the kernel decomposition of the cyclotomic factors of $X^N - 1$ acting on $C$:*

$$G = \ker(C - I), \qquad H = \ker \Phi^*(C).$$

*Proof.* For the first identity: $(C - I)v = 0$ iff $Cv = v$, iff $v$ has all entries equal under cyclic shift, iff $v \in \mathbb{R}\mathbf{1} = G$.

For the second: compute $\Phi^*(C) = \sum_{k=0}^{N-1} C^k$. The $(i,j)$ entry of $C^k$ is $1$ if $i \equiv j + k \pmod N$ and $0$ otherwise. Summing over $k = 0, \ldots, N-1$, each $(i,j)$ pair receives a contribution of exactly $1$ (from the unique $k = (i-j) \bmod N$). Hence

$$\Phi^*(C) = \mathbf{1}\mathbf{1}^\top = N \cdot P_G,$$

a rank-1 matrix. Its kernel is $\mathbf{1}^\perp = H$. $\square$

**Remark 3.1 (cyclotomic reading).** The factorization $X^N - 1 = (X - 1) \cdot \Phi^*(X)$ produces, by the structure theorem for finitely generated modules over the PID $\mathbb{Q}[X]$, a decomposition

$$\mathbb{Q}^N \;\cong\; \mathbb{Q}[X] / (X - 1) \;\oplus\; \mathbb{Q}[X] / \Phi^*(X) \;\cong\; G_{\mathbb{Q}} \oplus H_{\mathbb{Q}}$$

as $\mathbb{Q}[C]$-modules. The gauge direction *is* the trivial cyclotomic factor; the zero-sum hyperplane *is* the higher cyclotomic content. This is the central reading of the paper: gauge ↔ $\Phi_1$, zero-sum ↔ $\Phi^* = \prod_{d > 1} \Phi_d$.

**Remark 3.2 (gauge as symmetrization).** The projection $P_G = \tfrac{1}{N} \Phi^*(C)$ is the *averaging* (or trivial-character projection) of the cyclic action. In Maschke's-theorem language [Serre 1977, §1.2], $G$ is the trivial isotypic component of the regular representation of $\mathbb{Z}/N$ on $\mathbb{R}^N$, and $H$ is the sum of all non-trivial isotypic components. The cyclotomic factorization of $\Phi^*$ is then the further decomposition of $H$ into isotypic components of distinct order.

## 4. The case of prime $N$

**Theorem 2 (prime-$N$ cyclotomic field structure).** *Let $N$ be prime. Then $\mathbb{Q}[C]|_H$ is a field, isomorphic to the cyclotomic field $\mathbb{Q}(\zeta_N)$, and $H_{\mathbb{Q}}$ is a free rank-one module over this field. The action of $C$ on $H_{\mathbb{Q}}$ corresponds, after choosing any non-zero generator, to multiplication by $\zeta_N$:*

$$\mathbb{Q}[C]|_H \;\cong\; \mathbb{Q}(\zeta_N), \qquad H_{\mathbb{Q}} \;\cong\; \mathbb{Q}(\zeta_N) \text{ as a } \mathbb{Q}[C]\text{-module}.$$

*The latter isomorphism is not canonical: it depends on a choice of cyclic generator of $H_{\mathbb{Q}}$ over $\mathbb{Q}[C]|_H$. The action of $C$ has no proper non-zero $\mathbb{Q}[C]$-invariant submodule.*

*Proof.* By Theorem 1, $C|_H$ has minimal polynomial dividing $\Phi^*(X) = \Phi_N(X)$. Since $\Phi_N$ is irreducible over $\mathbb{Q}$ and $C \neq I$ on $H$, the minimal polynomial of $C|_H$ is exactly $\Phi_N$. Hence $\mathbb{Q}[C]|_H \cong \mathbb{Q}[X]/\Phi_N(X) \cong \mathbb{Q}(\zeta_N)$, a field. Any non-zero vector $v \in H_{\mathbb{Q}}$ generates a $\mathbb{Q}[C]$-submodule of dimension equal to $\deg \Phi_N = N - 1 = \dim_{\mathbb{Q}} H_{\mathbb{Q}}$, so $H_{\mathbb{Q}}$ is cyclic and free of rank one over $\mathbb{Q}(\zeta_N)$. Different choices of $v$ give different field isomorphisms $H_{\mathbb{Q}} \cong \mathbb{Q}(\zeta_N)$ that differ by multiplication by elements of $\mathbb{Q}(\zeta_N)^\times$. $\square$

**Corollary 4.1.** *For $N$ prime, the rational zero-sum hyperplane carries a canonical structure of a rank-one module over $\mathbb{Q}(\zeta_N)$. Translating the simplicial frame data to a field structure on $H_{\mathbb{Q}}$ requires an additional non-canonical choice of cyclic generator.*

**Examples.**

- *$N = 2$:* $\Phi_2(X) = X + 1$. $H_2 = \{(t, -t) : t \in \mathbb{R}\}$ is 1-dimensional; $C$ acts as $-I$. The "cyclotomic field" $\mathbb{Q}(\zeta_2) = \mathbb{Q}$.
- *$N = 3$:* $\Phi_3(X) = X^2 + X + 1$. $\mathbb{Q}(\zeta_3) = \mathbb{Q}(\omega)$ (Eisenstein field). $H_3$ is 2-dimensional; $C$ acts as multiplication by $\omega = e^{2\pi i/3}$. Treated in detail in §5.
- *$N = 5$:* $\Phi_5$ has degree 4. $H_5 \cong \mathbb{Q}(\zeta_5)$ realified, 4-dimensional.
- *$N = 7$:* $\Phi_7$ has degree 6. $H_7 \cong \mathbb{Q}(\zeta_7)$ realified, 6-dimensional.

### 4.1 Real decomposition for prime $N \geq 3$

While $\Phi_N$ is irreducible over $\mathbb{Q}$, it factors over $\mathbb{R}$ for $N \geq 3$ into real quadratic factors, since the roots of $\Phi_N$ come in complex-conjugate pairs:

$$\Phi_N(X) = \prod_{k=1}^{(N-1)/2} \left( X^2 - 2\cos\!\tfrac{2\pi k}{N}\, X + 1 \right) \qquad (N \text{ odd prime}).$$

Each quadratic factor corresponds to a complex-conjugate pair $\{e^{2\pi i k / N}, e^{-2\pi i k / N}\}$ and represents a 2-plane on which $C$ acts as rotation by $2\pi k / N$.

**Theorem 2'.** *For prime $N \geq 3$, $H$ decomposes orthogonally over $\mathbb{R}$ into $(N-1)/2$ irreducible 2-planes,*

$$H = \bigoplus_{k=1}^{(N-1)/2} H^{(k)}, \qquad \dim_{\mathbb{R}} H^{(k)} = 2,$$

*with $C|_{H^{(k)}}$ acting as rotation by $2\pi k / N$. The decomposition is preserved by $\langle C \rangle$.*

For $N = 3$, this sum has a single term, so $H_3$ is itself a single 2-plane. For $N \geq 5$ prime, $H$ is genuinely higher-dimensional and contains multiple distinct rotation rates simultaneously.

## 5. The $N = 3$ self-cyclotomic uniqueness

We now isolate the geometric peculiarity of $N = 3$.

**Theorem 3 ($N = 3$ uniqueness).** *Let $N \geq 2$. The cyclic permutation $C$ acts irreducibly on $H$ as a real $\mathbb{R}[C]$-module of dimension $\geq 2$ if and only if $N = 3$.*

*Proof.* If $N$ is composite, Theorem 4 below decomposes $H_{\mathbb{Q}}$ into $\geq 2$ proper $\mathbb{Q}[C]$-invariant submodules; extension of scalars to $\mathbb{R}$ preserves the decomposition, so $C|_H$ is reducible over $\mathbb{R}$. If $N$ is an odd prime $\geq 5$, Theorem 2' decomposes $H$ over $\mathbb{R}$ into $(N-1)/2 \geq 2$ proper invariant 2-planes. If $N = 2$, $H_2$ is 1-dimensional, so the dimension-$\geq 2$ condition fails. The only remaining case is $N = 3$, where $H_3$ is 2-dimensional and $C|_{H_3}$ has irreducible real minimal polynomial $\Phi_3(X) = X^2 + X + 1$. $\square$

**Corollary 5.1 (simplex-cyclotomy coincidence).** *The centered vertices of the regular $(N-1)$-simplex coincide, as a planar configuration in some real 2-plane, with the $N$-th roots of unity if and only if $N = 3$.*

*Proof.* The centered vertices form a single $\langle C \rangle$-orbit of unit vectors in $H$ under the simplicial inner product. They lie in a real 2-plane iff $\dim_{\mathbb{R}} H = 2$, i.e., iff $N = 3$. $\square$

### 5.1 Explicit identification at $N = 3$

The centered vertices of the regular triangular simplex are

$$\hat f_1 = \tfrac{1}{3}(2, -1, -1), \quad \hat f_2 = \tfrac{1}{3}(-1, 2, -1), \quad \hat f_3 = \tfrac{1}{3}(-1, -1, 2),$$

with $\hat f_1 + \hat f_2 + \hat f_3 = 0$ and (by direct computation) $\langle \hat f_i, \hat f_i \rangle_s = 1$ under the simplicial Gram metric $G_s = \tfrac{3}{2} I - \tfrac{1}{2} \mathbf{1}\mathbf{1}^\top$. They form a single $\langle C \rangle$-orbit:

$$C \hat f_1 = \hat f_2, \quad C \hat f_2 = \hat f_3, \quad C \hat f_3 = \hat f_1.$$

Equip $H_3$ with the complex structure $J : H_3 \to H_3$ characterized uniquely (up to sign / orientation) by

$$C|_{H_3} = -\tfrac{1}{2} I_{H_3} + \tfrac{\sqrt{3}}{2} J,$$

i.e., $J$ is the simplicial $\pi/2$-rotation generator from [Murillo-Montero 2026], satisfying $J^2 = -I_{H_3}$. The pair $(H_3, J)$ becomes a 1-dimensional complex vector space, and the choice $\hat f_1 \mapsto 1$ supplies a $\mathbb{C}$-linear isomorphism

$$H_3 \;\xrightarrow{\;\sim\;}\; \mathbb{C}, \qquad \hat f_k \;\longmapsto\; \omega^{k - 1} \quad (k = 1, 2, 3),$$

where $\omega = e^{2\pi i / 3}$. The action of $C$ on $H_3$ corresponds to multiplication by $\omega$ on $\mathbb{C}$.

**This is the explicit form of the self-cyclotomic theorem at $N = 3$.** The simplicial frame, the cyclotomic root configuration, and the cyclic group $\mathbb{Z}/3$ acting by multiplication coincide as a single object — and only at $N = 3$. The cube-root-of-unity sum identity $1 + \omega + \omega^2 = 0$ is, under this identification, the simplicial zero-sum identity $\hat f_1 + \hat f_2 + \hat f_3 = 0$.

## 6. Composite $N$: multi-cyclotomic decomposition

For composite $N$, the cyclotomic factorization of $\Phi^*(X) = (X^N - 1)/(X - 1)$ has multiple factors:

$$\Phi^*(X) = \prod_{d \,|\, N,\; d > 1} \Phi_d(X).$$

**Theorem 4 (composite-$N$ decomposition).** *For any $N \geq 2$, the rational zero-sum hyperplane decomposes as a direct sum of $\mathbb{Q}[C]$-modules,*

$$H_{\mathbb{Q}} = \bigoplus_{d \,|\, N,\; d > 1} H^{[d]}_{\mathbb{Q}}, \qquad H^{[d]}_{\mathbb{Q}} := \ker \Phi_d(C)|_{H_{\mathbb{Q}}},$$

*with $\dim_{\mathbb{Q}} H^{[d]}_{\mathbb{Q}} = \phi(d)$, $\sum_{d | N, d > 1} \phi(d) = N - 1$, and each summand $H^{[d]}_{\mathbb{Q}}$ a free rank-one module over $\mathbb{Q}(\zeta_d)$.*

*Proof.* By Theorem 1, $H_{\mathbb{Q}} = \ker \Phi^*(C)|_{\mathbb{Q}^N}$. The factors $\{\Phi_d : d | N, d > 1\}$ are pairwise coprime (cyclotomic polynomials of distinct order share no roots), so by the Chinese Remainder Theorem,

$$\mathbb{Q}[X] / \Phi^*(X) \;\cong\; \prod_{d \,|\, N,\; d > 1} \mathbb{Q}[X] / \Phi_d(X) \;=\; \prod_{d \,|\, N,\; d > 1} \mathbb{Q}(\zeta_d).$$

Translating to $\mathbb{Q}[C]$-modules via $H_{\mathbb{Q}} \cong \mathbb{Q}[C]|_H \cong \mathbb{Q}[X]/\Phi^*(X)$ and applying the totient identity $\sum_{d | N, d > 1} \phi(d) = N - 1$, the decomposition follows. $\square$

**Examples.**

- *$N = 4$:* $X^4 - 1 = \Phi_1 \Phi_2 \Phi_4 = (X-1)(X+1)(X^2 + 1)$. So $H_4$ splits as $H^{[2]} \oplus H^{[4]}$, with $\dim H^{[2]} = 1$ ($C$ acts as $-I$) and $\dim H^{[4]} = 2$ ($C$ acts as a $\pi/2$-rotation). The 4-simplex (tetrahedron) vertices live in this 3D zero-sum hyperplane: a 1D negation plus a 2D quarter-turn rotation, *not* a single planar 4th-root-of-unity orbit.
- *$N = 6$:* $X^6 - 1 = \Phi_1 \Phi_2 \Phi_3 \Phi_6$. $\dim H_6 = 5 = 1 + 2 + 2$, decomposing as a $\mathbb{Z}/2$-piece, a $\mathbb{Z}/3$-piece (Eisenstein), and a primitive $\mathbb{Z}/6$-piece (Eisenstein with reversed orientation).
- *$N = 8$:* $X^8 - 1 = \Phi_1 \Phi_2 \Phi_4 \Phi_8$. $\dim H_8 = 7 = 1 + 2 + 4$, with a $\mathbb{Z}/2$-piece, a $\pi/2$-rotation, and a 4-dimensional primitive $\zeta_8$-action.

In every composite case, $H$ acquires a non-irreducible structure that distinguishes it sharply from the prime case. The simplicial frame at composite $N$ carries multiple cyclotomic clocks running at distinct rates simultaneously.

## 7. Discussion

### 7.1 Number-theoretic content of simplicial frames

The decomposition results above place the simplicial coordinate framework into direct contact with cyclotomic number theory. For prime $N$, the rational zero-sum hyperplane $H_{\mathbb{Q}}$ is canonically a rank-one module over the cyclotomic field $\mathbb{Q}(\zeta_N)$, with the cyclic frame symmetry acting as field multiplication. The Galois group $\mathrm{Gal}(\mathbb{Q}(\zeta_N) / \mathbb{Q}) \cong (\mathbb{Z}/N)^\times$ acts on the field by sending $\zeta_N \mapsto \zeta_N^k$ for $\gcd(k, N) = 1$; correspondingly, replacing $C$ with $C^k$ for any $k$ coprime to $N$ produces the same $\mathbb{Q}[C]$-module structure on $H_{\mathbb{Q}}$ but a different generator of the cyclotomic action.

The $\mathbb{Q}$-vs-$\mathbb{R}$ distinction is essential. The prime-vs-composite contrast in this paper is a contrast of irreducibility *over $\mathbb{Q}$*: $\Phi_N$ is irreducible over $\mathbb{Q}$ for prime $N$ (Theorem 2) and reducible for composite $N$ (Theorem 4). Over $\mathbb{R}$, the picture is uniform once $N \geq 3$: every $\Phi_d$ with $d \geq 3$ factors into real quadratics indexed by complex-conjugate pairs of roots, so for prime $N \geq 5$, $H$ already splits over $\mathbb{R}$ into $(N-1)/2$ irreducible 2-planes (Theorem 2'). The case $N = 3$ is the unique value at which the $\mathbb{Q}$-irreducibility and the $\mathbb{R}$-irreducibility coincide.

For composite $N$, the divisor decomposition of Theorem 4 corresponds to the decomposition of the group ring $\mathbb{Q}[\mathbb{Z}/N]$ as a product of cyclotomic fields — Maschke's theorem for the abelian group $\mathbb{Z}/N$ over $\mathbb{Q}$, made concrete in simplicial coordinates.

### 7.2 The $N = 3$ exceptionality, paired with the $N = 4$ exceptionality

Theorem 3 identifies $N = 3$ as the unique non-degenerate value at which the simplicial frame, the cyclotomic roots-of-unity polygon, and the rotational planar geometry coincide. This pairs cleanly with the result of [Murillo-Montero 2026], where $N = 4$ is identified as the unique value at which the simplicial wedge-Hodge construction yields a single-axis binary cross product parameterizing all of $\mathfrak{so}(3)$ — itself a reflection of the exceptional Lie-algebra isomorphism $\mathfrak{so}(3) \cong (\mathbb{R}^3, \times)$ and the Eckmann classification.

The two distinguished cases sit in distinct algebraic worlds:

- **$N = 3$ — the cyclotomic / planar exception.** The cyclic permutation $C$ acts on the 2-dimensional zero-sum hyperplane $H_3$ with irreducible real minimal polynomial $\Phi_3(X) = X^2 + X + 1$. The vertex frame, the cube roots of unity, and the discrete cyclic group $\mathbb{Z}/3$ acting by planar rotation coincide as a single object. This is a *finite-group / cyclotomic* exception.
- **$N = 4$ — the Lie / cross-product exception.** The 3-dimensional zero-sum hyperplane $H_4$ admits an intrinsic binary cross product, and the Rodrigues exponential of the resulting skew operator parameterizes all of $\mathrm{SO}(3)$. This is a *continuous Lie-algebra / Hodge* exception.

These two exceptions are independent. The $N = 3$ exception does not extend to higher $N$ in the cyclotomic sense (Theorem 3); the $N = 4$ exception does not extend to higher $N$ in the cross-product sense [Murillo-Montero 2026, §8]. They are paired only by the broader simplicial framework, which selects them as the two values at which the gauge quotient $H$ carries a particularly clean algebraic structure — discrete in the $N = 3$ case, continuous in the $N = 4$ case.

For prime $N \geq 5$, the simplex vertices and the $N$-th roots of unity remain in a single $\langle C \rangle$-orbit and remain related by Theorem 2 (both encoded as elements of a rank-one $\mathbb{Q}(\zeta_N)$-module), but they no longer lie in a common 2-plane. For composite $N$, the picture decouples further across multiple cyclotomic components.

### 7.3 Open directions

Three directions follow naturally from this framework. We state them as future work; none is settled here.

**(a) Lattice structure.** For prime $N$, the standard real embedding of $\mathbb{Q}(\zeta_N)$ via Minkowski produces a cyclotomic lattice from the ring of integers $\mathbb{Z}[\zeta_N]$. The integral simplicial points $\mathbb{Z}^N \cap H$, however, are *not* canonically the cyclotomic lattice $\mathbb{Z}[\zeta_N]$: they form the augmentation lattice $A_{N-1} = \{(z_1, \ldots, z_N) \in \mathbb{Z}^N : \sum z_i = 0\}$, which is the standard root lattice of type $A_{N-1}$. For $N = 3$, $A_2$ and $\mathbb{Z}[\zeta_3]$ are isomorphic as lattices (both hexagonal), but for prime $N \geq 5$ they differ — $A_{N-1}$ sits inside the cyclotomic lattice with non-trivial index, and the relationship is governed by the discriminant of $\mathbb{Q}(\zeta_N)$. A precise comparison — including the Gram form on $H$, the trace form on $\mathbb{Q}(\zeta_N)$, and the proportionality constant on the irreducible $\mathbb{Q}[C]$-component — would clarify the integral content of the simplicial framework and connect it to lattice-based number theory.

**(b) The two exceptions in interaction.** The continuous Lie-algebra structure of [Murillo-Montero 2026] and the discrete cyclotomic structure of the present paper coexist on the same hyperplane $H$ but encode different algebraic data. The cyclic permutation $C|_H$ sits in $SO(H)$ only for odd $N$; for even $N$ it is orientation-reversing, and only its even powers $\langle C^2 \rangle$ lie in $SO(H)$ (Proposition 2.2).

The natural ambient setting is the orthogonal group $O(H)$, equipped with the distinguished finite label-symmetry subgroup $\rho(S_N) \leq O(H)$ — the Coxeter / Weyl group of type $A_{N-1}$, realized as the standard reflection representation of $S_N$ on the zero-sum hyperplane. The cyclic subgroup $\langle C \rangle \cong \mathbb{Z}/N$ is one selected copy inside this Weyl group; together, $SO(H)$ together with $C$ generates $SO(H)$ for $N$ odd and $O(H)$ for $N$ even. We do *not* form an external semidirect product $O(H) \rtimes \mathbb{Z}/N$, since $\rho(\mathbb{Z}/N)$ is already inside $O(H)$ and such a construction would double-count the cyclic action.

The cyclic-specific compatibility questions are governed instead by the **centralizer** $Z_{O(H)}(C)$ and **normalizer** $N_{O(H)}(\langle C \rangle)$. The centralizer asks what continuous orthogonal transformations commute with the cyclotomic clock; the normalizer asks what transformations preserve the clock up to relabeling its generator $C \mapsto C^k$ for $\gcd(k, N) = 1$. The standard $N/Z$ embedding gives

$$N_{O(H)}(\langle C \rangle) \,/\, Z_{O(H)}(\langle C \rangle) \;\hookrightarrow\; \mathrm{Aut}(\langle C \rangle) \;=\; (\mathbb{Z}/N)^\times,$$

placing the Galois group of $\mathbb{Q}(\zeta_N)/\mathbb{Q}$ inside the orthogonal symmetry of $H$ as the action that permutes generators of the cyclic clock. The determination of this embedding (in particular, whether it is surjective, and how it interacts with the cyclotomic decomposition $H = \bigoplus H^{[d]}$) is left to future work.

The centralizer itself can be made explicit. The decomposition of $H$ over $\mathbb{R}$ into $\Phi_d$-eigencomponents — 1-dimensional for $d \in \{1, 2\}$ and 2-dimensional otherwise — restricts $Z_{O(H)}(C)$ to a product of orthogonal groups acting on the individual eigencomponents and commuting with the rotation rate on each:

$$Z_{O(H)}(C) \;\cong\; \begin{cases} (S^1)^{(N-1)/2} & N \text{ odd}, \\ O(1) \times (S^1)^{N/2 - 1} & N \text{ even}. \end{cases}$$

The $O(1)$ factor in the even case is exactly the $\Phi_2$ component, where $C$ acts as $-I$ and any $\pm 1$ scaling commutes with it. We refer to this 1-dimensional component as the *parity line* (or *alternating-contrast axis*) of the simplicial frame; it is distinct from, and complementary to, the gauge axis $\mathbb{R}\mathbf{1}$, and exists only for even $N$.

This reframes the $N = 3$ exceptionality (Theorem 3) in a particularly clean form:

> *$N = 3$ is the unique value with $\dim H \geq 2$ for which $Z_{O(H)}(C)$ acts transitively on the unit sphere of $H$.*

For $N = 3$, the centralizer is $SO(2)$, which acts transitively on $S^1$ — the unit sphere of $H_3$. For $N \geq 5$, the centralizer is a torus that preserves the radii on each cyclotomic 2-plane separately, so it cannot mix between distinct rotation rates and is therefore not transitive on the unit sphere of $H$. The transitivity at $N = 3$ is the continuous-symmetry expression of the fact that the cube roots of unity exhaust the planar rotational structure: there is no second cyclotomic plane to keep separate from the first.

The present paper concerns the linear gauge-quotient picture only. If translations are introduced — i.e., for affine simplicial geometry or its integral lattice version — the natural ambient object changes: continuous affine geometry takes the form $H \rtimes O(H)$, and the integral / root-lattice version is the affine Weyl group $\widehat{W}(A_{N-1}) = Q(A_{N-1}) \rtimes S_N$, where $Q(A_{N-1}) = \mathbb{Z}^N \cap H$ is the root lattice. This affine extension is independent of the present development.

**(c) Composite $N$ as nested clocks.** Theorem 4 represents composite $N$ as a tower of cyclotomic factors $\{\Phi_d : d | N, d > 1\}$, each contributing a distinct rotation rate $2\pi/d$ on a distinct $\mathbb{Q}[C]$-isotypic component of $H$. The simplicial frame at composite $N$ thus carries a "nested clocks" structure that may admit applications to multi-rate signal processing and the analysis of compound cyclic systems, without committing to any particular such application.
