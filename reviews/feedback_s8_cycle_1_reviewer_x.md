## §8 Cycle 1 Formalism Audit — Reviewer X

**Scope.** §8 "Higher Dimensions and Future Work" (lines 453–481), cross-checked against §§1–7 + the new references introduced in external cycle 1 and §7 cycle 2.

### Findings

---

#### High — Severity

**H1. §8.1 (line 459): "Skew operators of rank greater than 2" is factually wrong; this undermines the stated obstruction to higher $N$.**

> "For $N \geq 5$, the analogous construction — taking Hodge duals of wedge products on the zero-sum hyperplane — produces skew operators of rank greater than 2, whose minimal polynomials are higher-degree and do not admit the same clean trigonometric refolding."

The "analogous construction" is then made explicit in §8.2 (line 465): for $k = N-3$ vectors $u_1, \dots, u_{N-3} \in H$, form $\star(u_1 \wedge \cdots \wedge u_{N-3})$, a 2-form, and contract with a vector. But a wedge of vectors is *simple* by construction, and the Hodge dual of a simple $r$-form in an oriented inner-product space is a simple $(\dim H - r)$-form. A simple 2-form is *decomposable* as $a \wedge b$, which corresponds to a **rank-2** skew operator $v \mapsto \langle a,v\rangle b - \langle b,v\rangle a$. Thus the higher-$N$ simplicial generator built via wedge-and-Hodge is **rank 2**, not "rank greater than 2," for all $N \geq 4$; in particular, its minimal polynomial is still $x(x^2 + c^2)$ and it admits the same trigonometric refolding $e^{\theta K}$ as for $N = 4$.

The real obstruction to a single closed-form Rodrigues exponentiation in higher $N$ is **not** rank of a particular generator — it is exactly the Lie-theoretic point the *next* paragraph (line 461) already makes correctly: $\dim \mathfrak{so}(N-1) = \binom{N-1}{2} > N-1$ for $N \geq 5$, so a single $(N-1)$-dim axis argument cannot parametrize $\mathfrak{so}(N-1)$. Generic elements of $\mathfrak{so}(N-1)$ are sums of rank-2 "blade" generators with non-commuting supports, and those do not share eigenspaces, so the exponential does not refold to a single closed form even though each rank-2 summand does. The §8.1 paragraph should be rewritten so it invokes this Lie-algebraic obstruction (which is already correctly given in the very next paragraph) rather than the incorrect "higher-rank generator" story. As written, the two paragraphs give inconsistent reasons for the same conclusion.

**H2. §8.1 (line 459): "Rotations in $\mathrm{SO}(N-1)$ for $N-1 \geq 4$ ... have fixed subspaces of dimension $N-3$" is false for generic rotations; the claim conflates "simple rotation" with "rotation."**

A rotation in $\mathrm{SO}(m)$ decomposes canonically as a direct sum of rotations on 2-planes plus an identity block; if there are $k$ nontrivial 2-plane blocks, the fixed subspace has dimension $m - 2k$. A generic rotation has $k = \lfloor m/2 \rfloor$, so the generic fixed subspace has dimension $0$ (if $m$ even) or $1$ (if $m$ odd). Concretely:

- $N = 5$, $m = 4$: generic fixed subspace has dim **0**, not $N-3 = 2$.
- $N = 6$, $m = 5$: generic fixed subspace has dim **1**, not $N-3 = 3$.
- $N = 7$, $m = 6$: generic fixed subspace has dim **0**, not $N-3 = 4$.

The value $N - 3 = (N-1) - 2$ is correct only for a *simple* rotation (single rank-2 block, i.e.\ the image of a rank-2 generator under $\exp$). The text's grammatical subject is "Rotations in $\mathrm{SO}(N-1)$," which is a blanket statement and therefore wrong. Fix: restrict the claim to "Rotations generated by a *single* simplicial rank-2 generator (i.e., simple rotations in $\mathrm{SO}(N-1)$) have fixed subspaces of dimension $N-3$; this is strictly less than the $N-2$ that would be required for a single 'axis' generalizing the 3D case," or similar. This also dovetails with H1 above.

#### Medium — Severity

**M1. §8.1 (line 461): Uniqueness phrasing omits the "within our framework" hedge that §1.2 item 6 carefully supplied.**

> "The uniqueness of $N = 4$ for the binary cross product with closed-form exponentiation is the simplicial-coordinate reflection of the exceptional Lie-algebra isomorphism $\mathfrak{so}(3) \cong (\mathbb{R}^3, \times)$."

§1.2 item 6 (line 61) explicitly qualifies uniqueness as holding "within the simplicial / Hodge-dual / axis-to-skew-operator framework developed here," and flags the $n=7$ octonion cross product as a binary case lying *outside* that framework. §8.1 drops this qualification and asserts bare "uniqueness of $N = 4$ for the binary cross product," which a reader encountering §8 in isolation may read as a global uniqueness claim contradicting Eckmann's classification into $n \in \{0,1,3,7\}$. Add the same "within our simplicial wedge-Hodge framework" hedge that §1.2 uses, or explicitly cross-reference §1.2 item 6.

**M2. §8.3 Rational Trigonometry (line 473): "Wildberger's Weierstrass parameter" mis-attributes the substitution.**

The Weierstrass substitution $t = \tan(\theta/2)$ is classical (19th century) and is not Wildberger's invention. Wildberger's program of rational trigonometry [Wildberger] replaces angle $\theta$ with *spread* $s = \sin^2 \theta$ and *quadrance* $Q = d^2$; the Weierstrass substitution is a compatible tool for passing between trigonometric and rational forms, but the phrasing "Wildberger's Weierstrass parameter" suggests authorship. Rephrase as, e.g., "Substituting the Weierstrass parameter $t = \tan(\theta/2)$ into our formula — in the spirit of Wildberger's program of rational trigonometry [Wildberger] — yields …"

**M3. §8.3 (line 477): "Axes belonging to the tetrahedral symmetry family" is not defined.**

No prior section introduces a "tetrahedral symmetry family" of axes. The parenthetical "(where the components of $u$ in zero-sum form rationalize against the $1/\sqrt{3}$ in $K$)" hints at the intended set, but the condition is not stated precisely. The cleanest concrete condition is: $u \in H$ is a unit simplicial axis whose entries lie in $\sqrt{3}\,\mathbb{Q}$, equivalently $\sqrt{3}\,u \in \mathbb{Q}^4$. Since this is labelled as a concrete open question ("Characterizing precisely which axes admit the rational parameterization"), it would help to state the candidate set concretely rather than via an undefined symmetry name.

**M4. §8.3 (line 481): "All exhibit the gauge-direction plus zero-sum-hyperplane structure" is asserted too strongly; "cognitive hyperdimensional computing" is uncited and the structural match is doubtful.**

Stoichiometric compatibility classes in chemical reaction network theory clearly have the zero-sum structure (conservation laws define the stoichiometric subspace) — [Müller-Regensburger] is already cited elsewhere and is apt. Analog gravity's connection to "gauge-direction plus zero-sum-hyperplane" is considerably more tenuous; the field's natural arena is Lorentzian effective metrics on condensed-matter backgrounds, not simplex gauge quotients. Cognitive hyperdimensional computing (Kanerva, Plate) uses hyperspheres or binary cubes, with element-wise products or circular convolutions; zero-sum structure is at most incidental. The paper asserts all three "exhibit" the shared structure, which is stronger than the "an open question" framing that closes the paragraph. Two concrete fixes:

- Downgrade "all exhibit" to "may admit" or "have been described in terms of …-like structures" with actual citations. A Kanerva reference is needed if HDC stays in the list (e.g., Kanerva, *Cognitive Computation* 2009).
- If a clean common structural claim cannot be defended, drop analog gravity and/or HDC from the list and retain the stoichiometric example alone.

As written, this is the single most "overclaim-ish" sentence in §8.

#### Low — Severity

**L1. §8.1 (line 457): Minimal-polynomial qualifier.**

> "rank-2 skew matrices have minimal polynomial $x(x^2 + c^2)$ for some constant $c$."

Strictly, a rank-2 real skew-symmetric matrix embedded in ambient dimension $\geq 3$ has minimal polynomial $x(x^2 + c^2)$; for ambient dimension $= 2$ (rank = ambient) the minimal polynomial is $x^2 + c^2$ (no zero factor). The immediately preceding clause establishes $K(u)$ is $4 \times 4$, so the statement is correct in context; but a sentence-level fix such as "rank-2 skew matrices with at least one zero eigenvalue have minimal polynomial $x(x^2 + c^2)$" would make the standalone claim precise.

**L2. §8.2 (line 465): Musical isomorphism is skipped.**

> "a 2-form, which when contracted against another vector produces a vector, i.e.\ a linear operator on $H$."

Contracting a 2-form with a vector yields a *1-form*. The identification with a vector requires the musical isomorphism $\sharp: H^* \to H$ induced by the simplicial inner product. For a "(sketch)" subsection this is acceptable shorthand, but a half-sentence acknowledgement — e.g., "contracted against another vector and raised via the simplicial metric produces a vector" — would align with the level of care taken in §3.4 and §4.

**L3. §8.3 (line 479): Ilr / clr factorization is correct, but the prose reads ambiguously on first pass.**

The factorization ilr = (orthonormal basis on $H$) $\circ\ c^{\mathrm{zs}} \circ \log$ is correct: clr is log composed with the zero-sum projection, and ilr is clr followed by an orthonormal basis change $H \to \mathbb{R}^{N-1}$. The sentence reads fine on a second pass, but "composition of the component-wise log map with the zero-sum projection … followed by a choice of orthonormal basis" has a mild left-to-right ambiguity. Optional clarification: "the composition of the orthonormal-basis isometry $H \to \mathbb{R}^{N-1}$, the zero-sum projection $c \mapsto c^{\mathrm{zs}}$ of §2.3 (whose precomposition with log is the centered log-ratio), and the component-wise log." Not required for correctness.

**L4. §8.3 (line 479): "Pulls back to the Fisher information metric."**

The Fisher metric on the probability simplex in coordinates $p_i$ is $ds^2 = \sum dp_i^2/p_i$; under $x_i = \sqrt{p_i}$ this becomes $4 \sum dx_i^2$ restricted to $\sum x_i^2 = 1$, i.e., 4 times the spherical metric. The simplicial inner product on $H$ carries an additional factor of $N/(N-1)$ from Proposition 2.2. So "pulls back to the Fisher information metric" holds only up to a positive scalar. Readers familiar with information geometry will read this charitably (Fisher is defined up to positive rescaling anyway), so flagging only as Low. An "up to a constant scaling" parenthetical would be more precise.

**L5. §8.3 (line 471): Implicit use of Prop 6.1.**

> "The product of two rotation matrices $R(u_1,\theta_1)\,R(u_2,\theta_2)$ is again a rotation matrix, necessarily of the form $R(u_3,\theta_3)$."

Justification chain: by Prop 6.1 items 1, 2, 6, and 8, each $R(u_i,\theta_i)$ lies in $\mathrm{SO}(4) \cap \mathrm{Stab}(\mathbf{1})$; the intersection is a subgroup, so the product lies in it; restricted to $H$ the product is an element of $\mathrm{SO}(3)$, hence axis-angle, hence of the claimed form after lifting back. "Necessarily" is justified, but a one-word reference ("by Proposition 6.1") would make the assertion traceable.

**L6. Typography.** "Fisher–Rao" at line 479 uses a proper en-dash (U+2013). All "higher-$N$," "rank-2," "component-wise," "zero-sum-hyperplane" compounds use hyphens correctly. "wedge-Hodge," "wedge-and-Hodge" internal consistency could be tightened (both appear elsewhere in the paper), but §8 itself is internally consistent. No dangling @-refs. All four newly introduced references ([Arnold-Falk-Winther], [Desbrun-Hirani-Leok-Marsden], [Pawlowsky-Glahn-Egozcue], [Shoemake]) are present in the bibliography and match the body citations.
